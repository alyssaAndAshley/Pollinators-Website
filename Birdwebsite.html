Birds

Birds come in all different shapes, colors and sizes. There are over 10,000 species of birds worldwide. About 2,000 of them are pollinating birds. These birds are crucial to their ecosystems, because they've evolved right alongside the plants there, and are specially suited for them. A majority of these pollinating birds have long beaks, that are mainly curved so that they can reach the nectar inside of the different flowers. Most of the pollinating birds are abundant in the more topic areas. Although there is no crop in the North American continent that needs bird pollinators, wild flowers and crops in the tropic do. Without these pollinators we may not have the wide variety of crops and foods to choose from.

Most of these pollinating birds like flowers that are bright in color, usually red, odorless, they must be strong enough for the birds to land on, and open for during the day time. Most pollinating birds have long beaks that are curved so that they can reach inside of the flower and retrieve the nectar. By doing this they end up getting pollen on their beak and as they go from flower to flower they end up pollinating. Some of the crops that need bird pollinators are papaya, and banana along with many others.

Hummingbirds

One of the most common of answers when most Americans are asked about a species of a pollinating bird will be a hummingbird. Hummingbirds are typically small, in the three inch to five inch range, and have slim beaks. Some hummingbirds can even travel up to 3,000 miles a year. What is even more amazing is that they can flap their wings over 80 times in the amount of time it takes for you to blink.

Hummingbirds in the Midwest

In the United States of America there are almost a dozen species under this genus. In the midwest region you might find Black-chinned, Calliope, Rufous and Broad-tailed hummingbirds, and Ruby-throated. They usually migrate from this area to more warmer areas for the winter, and usually will return again in April and May. These birds help to pollinate some of the crops in this region. These hummingbirds will typically eat nectar but also other flying insects (Have to keep that mosquito population down!)
