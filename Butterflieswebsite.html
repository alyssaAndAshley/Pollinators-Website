Butterflies

There are over 17,500 species of butterflies in the world and 750 species in the United States. Although many admire them for their beauty butterflies are highly important to pollination. Each pollinator has their own unique features that make them better at certain things. Butterflies are less effective at pollinating than bees since they have shorter legs and they are not as “fuzzy” as them. However, they are better for evenly distributing pollen across long distances since they migrate, better for high amounts of cross-pollination and have great vision.

Butterflies are able to see a multitude of colors like red which bees cannot, in addition they use UV rays to help locate and mark flowers. This allows them to be attracted to different flowers than other pollinators. When butterflies locate a flower that they view as a good food source, they land, and use their tongue to retrieve the nectar after tasting it with their feet. Some pollen gets attached or stuck onto their feet. The next time they land on a different flower, that flower will then be pollinated to a certain degree. Butterflies typically like flowers that are bright in color, are in clusters, have large amounts of nectar and are typically open during the day.

Monarch Butterflies

Monarch Butterflies are one of the most well known species in America since they are native. Their marvelous patterns seem to be mystic and marvelous to so many. Each pattern acts as a fingerprint, it helps to identify them. Monarchs take long journeys, they travel from the Northern United States of America and Canada to Mexico and the Southern United States of America. However, for all that they do, they are endangered. In certain areas of the United States there are not ample supplies of milkweed for them to breed and lay their eggs on. In addition, when they migrate to Mexico there are not large enough forests or enough forests to hibernate in. In some areas in Mexico, there was a 53% decrease of Monarch butterflies in certain places. If that was not bad enough,because of climate change, where they reside currently over the winter may have to be changed.

Milkweed although seen as a boring green plant by most humans, is an essential plant to Monarch Butterflies, it is what provides pupae and caterpillars protection and food. When the eggs of monarch butterflies hatch, they then become caterpillars. Those caterpillars need an abundant amount of milkweed in order to go into their next stage, becoming a pupa and forming a chrysalis. After they break out of that chrysalis they become a full fledged butterfly. Since the butterfly ate the milkweed, a plant that is toxic to most animals, they have some of these toxins in themself too, and can help to ward off other animals. If a predator eats a monarch butterfly, the consequences for them can be severe, when a monarch butterfly is digested it subsequently poisons the predator. Milkweed is necessary for a monarch's survival, and in many places where there is milkweed they use pesticides which can kill many of the caterpillars, leaving less pupa which results in fewer butterflies.

When Monarch butterflies travel, they usually will travel in very large colonies, sometimes reaching over 10 million. By travelling in these large numbers, they will be able to create heat when they are hibernating. Monarchs are one of the only species of butterfly that make two trips, one to the southern parts of North America where they over winter, and with each generation, they continue to travel more North. In a cycle, the first generation may travel to Mexico or southern California, and over winter there, they travel to the Midwest region which is where they will lay their eggs on milkweed. Those Monarchs then overwinter again in Mexico, and as this continues, the offspring continually go more North. Monarchs are fascinating creatures and very well known, however they are becoming eating quickly, they need our help.

Palos Verdes Blue

Palos Verdes Blue butterflies are highly endangered in the United States.It is mainly found in Los Angeles, in the Palos Verdes Peninsula and County. This species branched off of another butterfly named Silvery Blue. They were first thought to be discovered in the 1970s, although there are no estimations from that time or in the 1980s, scientists believe there were probably less than 300 individuals in the county. Palos Verdes Blue butterflies. As the years went on their food sources were almost completely gone, and numbers decreased, eventually reaching only 4-7 sightings. However, there has been a captive propagation effort in the area, and in 2005 there were reported to be 93 new additional butterflies and pupae.
